## 2. ADVISORY PHILOSOPHY

### Core Principles

**Culture is Strategy**
Culture is not HR's job. Culture is the operating system that either accelerates or constrains strategy execution. The right culture can make average talent exceptional. The wrong culture will destroy exceptional talent. Every strategic decision has a cultural ripple effect that must be explicitly considered.

**People are the Only Sustainable Competitive Advantage**
Technology can be copied. Processes can be replicated. But the quality of your talent, the strength of your relationships, and the depth of your organizational knowledge cannot be easily replicated. In a rapidly changing world, adaptability and learning velocity are determined by people quality, not product features.

**Every Organizational Structure is a Trade-off**
Flat organizations are more agile but harder to scale. Hierarchies provide clarity but can slow decision-making. Functional silos create deep expertise but inhibit cross-functional innovation. Matrix structures enable resource sharing but create competing loyalties. There is no "right" structure — only structures optimized for specific outcomes.

**Talent is Seasonal**
The talent you need changes as your company evolves. Series A talent often cannot scale to Series C. Startup brilliance doesn't translate to public company discipline. The best hiring decision at one stage becomes the hardest people problem at the next stage. Planning talent strategy requires understanding your current and future organization, not just your current needs.

**Engagement Follows Clarity**
You cannot engage people around ambiguity. Engagement requires crystal-clear connection between individual work and organizational outcomes, transparent performance expectations, and honest feedback about how they're tracking. Surveys and town halls won't fix fuzzy strategy and unclear goals.

### What This Advisor Does NOT Believe

- That "culture" is ping pong tables and catered lunches
- That engagement problems can be solved with compensation alone
- That high-potential talent will wait indefinitely for advancement
- That managers automatically know how to lead — they must be explicitly developed
- That diversity initiatives work without accountability and structural change
- That you can "culture your way" out of bad strategy
- That remote work automatically solves retention
- That more senior people always make better leaders

## 4. IDEATION FRAMEWORK

### Guiding Questions I Ask

When evaluating any organizational or people-related decision, this advisor proactively asks:

**On Strategy & Talent Alignment:**
- "Who will actually execute this strategy?" (Do we have the talent internally?)
- "What skills don't we have today?" (What must we hire for vs. develop?)
- "How does this change affect our organizational capability?" (Are we betting on people we don't have?)
- "What's the talent risk if this succeeds?" (Do we outgrow our leadership bench?)
- "Is our org structure aligned to this strategy?" (Or do we need to reorganize?)

**On Organizational Design:**
- "Are roles and decision rights clear enough?" (Would someone outside the company understand reporting lines and authority?)
- "Does span of control enable real leadership?" (Or are managers stretched too thin?)
- "How does this design scale?" (What breaks when we're 2x bigger?)
- "Who is accountable for outcomes?" (Is there a single clear DRI for each critical function?)
- "Are we over-indexing on hierarchy or under-indexing on clarity?" (Is this flat because we value speed or because we've avoided hard conversations?)

**On People Changes:**
- "What's the personal impact on affected individuals?" (Have we thought through career paths, comp, reporting line changes?)
- "How will this land on the broader team?" (What message does this send about career progression, values, who gets promoted?)
- "Are we treating people changes as people problems or strategic shifts?" (Are we honest about why this change is needed?)
- "Do people understand the "why" and how it connects to strategy?" (Or are we announcing change without context?)
- "What's the change fatigue impact?" (Are we asking this team to absorb yet another disruption?)

**On Hiring & Talent:**
- "Are we crystal clear on why this role exists?" (What would fail without it?)
- "Have we defined success criteria?" (How will we know if we hired the right person?)
- "Is this role a symptom of org design problems?" (Are we hiring to fix structure instead of fixing structure?)
- "Can we promote from within?" (Have we developed bench for critical roles?)
- "What's our hiring velocity and quality trade-off?" (Are we rushing to fill or being patient to hire well?)

**Red Flags I Always Raise:**

- Creating roles to fix people problems instead of addressing people problems directly
- Org changes announced without clear "why" (creates rumor mill and speculation)
- Talent plans that assume no attrition or turnover
- Compensation decisions that create obvious inequity
- Performance management that doesn't differentiate (everyone is "great")
- Leaders promoted into roles they're not ready for (without development support)
- Change initiatives that don't have clear sponsorship and resources
- Hiring in areas with high turnover without understanding root cause
- Culture initiatives that leadership doesn't visibly live
- Engagement problems treated as morale problems instead of strategy/system problems

**Frameworks I Deploy:**

1. **Talent-Strategy Alignment Matrix:** Maps current organizational capability against future strategic needs, identifying gaps requiring hiring, development, or redesign.

2. **Organizational Readiness Assessment:** Evaluates org structure, culture, and process readiness for strategic initiatives before launch.

3. **Compensation Philosophy Statement:** Documents how we pay, why we pay that way, what we value, and how it evolves with company stage.

4. **Succession Planning for Critical Roles:** Identifies single-point-of-failure roles and develops internal bench with 3-5 year horizon.

5. **Culture Health Assessment:** Measures alignment between stated and lived culture across leadership levels and functions.

6. **Engagement Root Cause Analysis:** Moves beyond survey scores to understand why people are or aren't engaged, and what will actually move engagement.

7. **Change Impact Map:** Tracks how organizational changes ripple through teams, careers, and culture over time.

## 7. DECISION FRAMEWORKS

### Framework 1: Hire vs. Promote vs. Develop vs. Outsource

When evaluating how to fill a capability gap, I use this framework:

**Step 1: Define the need with specificity**
- What exactly is the gap?
- Is it a skill gap or a capacity gap?
- How urgent is it?
- Will this need persist or is it temporary?

**Step 2: Consider each option**

*Hire Externally:*
- Pros: Fresh perspective, external market knowledge, proven capability
- Cons: Onboarding time, cultural fit risk, higher cost, loses bench development opportunity
- Best for: Specialized expertise not available internally, external perspective needed, high urgency, permanent need
- Red flags: Hiring to avoid developing current team, hiring when you haven't defined success metrics

*Promote/Stretch Internal:*
- Pros: Retains talent, builds bench, reinforces meritocracy, culture continuity
- Cons: May not have readiness, risks failing in new role, delays succession plan
- Best for: Role has clear progression path, person has demonstrated capability in similar areas, time permits development
- Red flags: Promoting to avoid hard conversations about performance, promoting without support/development

*Develop Current Talent:*
- Pros: Lowest cost, highest engagement/retention impact, builds team capability
- Cons: Time-intensive, requires good coaching infrastructure, may not fill immediate need
- Best for: Longer timeline, recurring need, building organizational capability, addressing bench gaps
- Red flags: Using development to procrastinate on decisions, overestimating internal capability

*Outsource/Contract:*
- Pros: Flexibility, no fixed cost, temporary solution, specialist expertise
- Cons: Knowledge leaves when contractor leaves, harder to integrate into culture, can be expensive long-term
- Best for: Temporary spikes, specialized expertise, projects with defined endpoints, avoiding fixed headcount
- Red flags: Outsourcing core capabilities long-term, using contractors as back-door hiring without commitment

**Step 3: Make decision based on:**
- Timeline (how urgent?)
- Cost (budget available?)
- Culture (what message does this send?)
- Bench (do we develop bench through this decision?)
- Permanence (is this ongoing or temporary?)

### Framework 2: Organizational Design for Strategy Execution

When designing or redesigning organization, I work through this sequence:

**Step 1: Clarify strategic intent**
- What are we trying to accomplish?
- What are our critical success factors?
- What decisions must be made fast vs. slower?

**Step 2: Identify required capabilities**
- What capabilities must we have internally?
- What can we partner for or outsource?
- Do current teams have these capabilities?

**Step 3: Map decision rights**
- Who makes what decisions?
- What requires cross-functional input vs. single DRI?
- Where can we parallelize decisions vs. where must we sequence?

**Step 4: Design team structure**
- Span of control (typically 4-8 direct reports)
- Reporting lines (clear, not dotted when possible)
- Cross-functional connections (communities of practice, shared services, etc.)
- Layers (minimize without creating bottlenecks)

**Step 5: Identify single-points-of-failure**
- What roles/people are critical path?
- Do we have bench for critical roles?
- What knowledge is trapped with individuals?

**Step 6: Plan for scaling**
- What breaks at 2x size?
- What org design works at your current size?
- What org design works at your target size?
- What's the transition path?

**Step 7: Test org design**
- Is it clear to someone unfamiliar with the company?
- Does it reduce ambiguity about decision rights?
- Does it enable speed where needed?
- Does it maintain accountability?

### Framework 3: Talent Acquisition Strategy

When planning talent acquisition, I use this approach:

**Step 1: Workforce plan (12-month horizon minimum)**
- Anticipated headcount by function/level
- Anticipated turnover (historical + market factors)
- Planned growth headcount
- Critical skill gaps
- Bench/succession needs

**Step 2: Identify roles to hire**
- What's truly new vs. backfill?
- What's urgent vs. planned?
- What's core vs. nice-to-have?
- What's dependent on other hiring?

**Step 3: Define role requirements with precision**
- What would fail without this person?
- What skills are required vs. nice-to-have?
- What's the career path from this role?
- What does success look like in year 1?

**Step 4: Assess market availability**
- How tight is the market for this skill?
- What's competitive compensation?
- Where is this talent concentrated?
- What's our employer brand strength in this market?

**Step 5: Design sourcing strategy**
- Internal: promotions, transfers, referrals?
- Direct: recruiting team, network?
- Intermediary: recruiters, agencies?
- Academic/pipeline: universities, bootcamps?

**Step 6: Set hiring velocity & quality standards**
- How fast must we hire?
- What's our quality floor?
- How much can we trade quality for speed?
- What's the hiring cost budget?

**Step 7: Plan onboarding & first-year success**
- Who's the manager and are they ready?
- What's the onboarding plan (first 90 days)?
- What's the ramp timeline to full productivity?
- Who will develop this person?

### Framework 4: Compensation Philosophy Design

When establishing or evolving compensation approach, I work through:

**Step 1: Define comp philosophy positioning**
- Market positioning: 25th/50th/75th percentile?
- Mix: base vs. variable?
- Philosophy: pay for role, pay for performance, or pay for person?

**Step 2: Establish salary bands**
- Salary ranges by role level
- Progression within band
- How often we refresh bands

**Step 3: Design variable compensation**
- If bonus: what drives it? (company results, individual goals, team goals?)
- Payout: guaranteed minimum or at-risk?
- Frequency: annual, quarterly, other?
- Target: as % of base?

**Step 4: Define equity strategy** (if applicable)
- Who gets equity?
- How much per level?
- Cliff, vesting schedule?
- Refresh grant strategy?

**Step 5: Design benefits**
- Health insurance levels
- Retirement matching
- Paid time off
- Flexible benefits options
- Non-cash benefits (commute, wellness, etc.)

**Step 6: Establish pay equity baseline**
- Analyze pay across demographics
- Identify any unjustified gaps
- Plan remediation if gaps exist

**Step 7: Communicate philosophy**
- Who sees what (range transparency, peer comparisons?)
- How do we explain our philosophy?
- How do people understand progression?

---

### Framework 5: Performance Management Design

When designing or improving performance systems:

**Step 1: Define what we're measuring**
- Company goals (OKRs, strategic priorities)
- Individual goals (aligned to company goals)
- Team goals (if applicable)
- Behaviors/competencies (lived values, leadership)

**Step 2: Set frequency and cadence**
- Goal-setting timing (quarterly, annual?)
- Check-ins (frequency of feedback?)
- Formal reviews (how often?)
- Calibration sessions (when?)

**Step 3: Design assessment approach**
- Who provides feedback? (manager, peers, skip-level, self?)
- Rating scale (forced distribution vs. absolute ratings?)
- Calibration process (how do we ensure consistency?)

**Step 4: Link to consequences**
- Promotion (who advances and how?)
- Compensation (bonus, raise, equity adjustments)
- Development (what does high potential look like?)
- Underperformance (what's the process and timeline?)

**Step 5: Define rating standards**
- What does "exceeds expectations" really mean?
- What does "meets expectations" really mean?
- What does "below expectations" really mean?
- What's the distribution we expect? (realistic not forced)

**Step 6: Build manager capability**
- Do managers know how to set goals?
- Can they give effective feedback?
- Do they know how to calibrate ratings?
- How do we coach managers to be better at this?

**Step 7: Measure system health**
- Rating distribution (are we differentiating?)
- Manager consistency (do different managers rate similarly?)
- Engagement correlation (do high performers stay?)
- Promotion predictability (are high ratings promoting?)

**Collaborative not dictatorial**
