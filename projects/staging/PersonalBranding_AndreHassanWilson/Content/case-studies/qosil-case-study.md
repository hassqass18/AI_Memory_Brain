# Case Study: Building a Market-Entry Consulting Practice from Zero in Kazakhstan
**Company:** Qosil (Service Management Division, Genius Co Support Services)
**Role:** Intern → Business Development → Director of Business Development
**Period:** 2018 – January 2025
**Market:** Republic of Kazakhstan, Central Asia

---

## The Situation

In 2018, Qosil — the service management arm of Genius Co Support Services — made a bold bet: enter the Kazakhstani market as a digitization consulting firm with virtually no local infrastructure, no existing relationships, and no playbook for the region. The company's foundation was a curated expert network and a vested member community, funded through the Genius Co ecosystem.

Andre "Hassan" Wilson joined as an intern and was selected not for credentials, but for coachability — a trait honed through years as a collegiate athlete — and an unusual ability to build trust quickly across diverse environments.

Neither Hassan nor the company's Chairman had ever set foot in Kazakhstan before arrival.

---

## The Problem

Entering a frontier market cold is a different category of challenge from expanding into an established one. There were no warm introductions, no cultural roadmap, and no proven sales motion for the region. The Kazakhstani business landscape in 2018 operated on relationship capital that took years to build — and the company had none.

The risks were existential: without establishing credibility and a viable revenue path within the first operating cycle, the Kazakhstan venture would collapse entirely.

At the same time, the opportunity was real. Kazakhstan had launched its **Digital Kazakhstan** national initiative in 2017 — an ambitious government-backed push to modernize the country's economy through technology. At the time Qosil entered the market, the initiative was approximately 10% complete, creating a wide-open window for digitization consultants with the right positioning.

The challenge was getting to that window before it closed — or before the company ran out of runway.

---

## What Was Done

**Learning the Machine Before Running It**

Hassan spent his first phase immersed in the Chairman's operating method — studying how trust was built without physical products, how community members were converted into champions, and what the company's underlying RevOps cadence actually was. This foundation shaped every subsequent decision.

**Applying Behavioral Psychology to Market Entry**

Rather than pursuing accounts randomly, Hassan identified a behavioral pattern within the member community: certain individuals acted as opinion leaders whose endorsement caused broader adoption. He mapped this dynamic onto the Kazakhstani market, targeting institutional relationships that would serve as credibility anchors — not just business partners.

This led to two foundational partnerships:
- **Zaman Bank** — the only Kazakhstani-owned Islamic financing institution in the country, providing access to a trust-heavy segment of the market
- A **top-tier national law firm** — providing legal infrastructure and market intelligence that accelerated deal qualification

These relationships weren't just deals. They were signals to the broader market that Qosil belonged.

**Thought Leadership at the National Level**

Hassan and the team forged a working relationship with the leadership of **Astana Hub** — the premier technology and startup hub in Central Asia and the epicenter of Kazakhstan's tech policy ecosystem. This positioned Qosil as a recognized voice in the country's digital transformation conversation, including the **2021 national initiative to export $1 billion in IT services**.

**Cracking the Market with Data-Driven Outreach**

In 2020, Hassan designed and executed a cold outreach campaign targeting Kazakhstani companies ripe for digitization. Cold email was an untapped channel in the market at the time — most firms hadn't been systematically approached this way.

The campaign was anchored by a survey engineered to surface latent demand: companies were invited to assess what it would take to become fully digitized. The results:

- **38% response rate** on cold outreach
- **69% close rate** among respondents

Converted prospects purchased digitization consultation engagements and digitization services — moving from awareness to paying clients through a structured, repeatable sales motion that Hassan owned end-to-end.

---

## The Outcome

Over a six-year operating period, Qosil grew from a zero-presence market entrant into a recognized digitization consulting practice in Central Asia:

- Built a **$500K+ average annual deal size** consulting pipeline from a cold start
- Established **institutional partnerships** with a national bank, a leading law firm, and the country's top technology hub
- Achieved **thought leadership positioning** within Kazakhstan's national digital transformation agenda
- Developed a **repeatable outreach-to-close system** with industry-leading conversion metrics in an untapped channel
- Grew Hassan's role from intern to **Director of Business Development** — a progression driven entirely by on-the-ground results in a market with no training wheels

---

## The Differentiator

Most professionals handed this assignment would have returned home. The obstacles were real: an unfamiliar language, an unfamiliar culture, no local network, and no guarantee the bet would pay off. For a young professional newly stepping into expat life, every signal pointed toward retreat.

Hassan stayed. He studied the environment, identified its patterns, and built a system that worked — not by importing an American playbook, but by observing the behavioral psychology of the market and designing a strategy around it.

The result was six years of sustained market presence, a leadership role earned through execution, and a set of skills — cross-cultural deal architecture, emerging market GTM, and enterprise RevOps — that no classroom produces.
