# Case Study: Turning a Material Shortage into a Competition Win at Africa's First Solar Decathlon
**Company:** Freedom Builders (Construction Division, MHJ Genius Co Support Services)
**Role:** Project Manager & Subject Matter Expert (Contracted)
**Event:** Solar Decathlon Africa 2019 — Ben Guerir, Morocco
**Duration:** 2 months (1 month pre-build sourcing + 1 month on-the-ground construction)
**Context:** First Solar Decathlon ever held on the African continent

---

## The Situation

The Solar Decathlon Africa 2019 was not just a competition — it was a landmark event. The first of its kind on the African continent, held in Ben Guerir, Morocco, it challenged university teams to design, build, and operate fully functional, energy-efficient homes judged across ten rigorous categories: Architecture, Engineering & Construction, Market Appeal, Comfort Conditions, Appliances, Sustainability, Home Life & Entertainment, Communication & Social Awareness, Electrical Energy Balance, and Innovation.

Freedom Builders — the construction arm of MHJ Genius Co Support Services — sponsored two competing universities, using the event as a high-visibility platform to establish market credibility and demonstrate what the company could deliver. The firm was still in its product development and market entry stage; this competition was its proof of concept.

Andre "Hassan" Wilson was brought in on a two-month contract as Project Manager and Subject Matter Expert. His mandate was clear: help the teams complete a build that would put Freedom Builders on the map.

What he inherited was anything but straightforward.

---

## The Problem

Two compounding challenges hit before the first brick was laid.

**Challenge 1: A Critical Supply Chain Failure**

The machinery needed to produce the bulk of the building materials on-site failed to clear customs. This wasn't a minor setback — it eliminated the primary production method for the structural materials the build depended on. The team arrived at the competition significantly behind before work had even begun.

**Challenge 2: Academic Teams Don't Run Like Construction Sites**

The sponsored teams were made up of university students and faculty — intelligent, motivated, and deeply knowledgeable about sustainable design theory. But academic environments operate on different norms than professional construction. Standard project management practices — phased timelines, labor sequencing, materials-to-task mapping — were not in place. Decision-making was slow, politically navigated, and consensus-driven in a context where speed and decisive prioritization were survival requirements.

The combination of material shortages and an unstructured team operating under an unforgiving deadline created a real risk of non-completion — an outcome that would have been publicly visible at one of the continent's most watched construction and innovation events.

---

## What Was Done

**Sourcing Around the Problem**

Unable to rely on the original production machinery, Hassan and his associate went to ground — identifying and securing masonry bricks from a local vendor in Ben Guerir. This shift required rapid supplier qualification, negotiation, and logistics coordination in an unfamiliar environment with a ticking clock. It also meant reconfiguring the entire build approach around what was now actually available.

**Building a Real Project Timeline — Mid-Crisis**

With materials and labor constraints now clearly defined, Hassan created a project timeline from scratch: task-sequenced, materials-dependent, and built around the actual labor available at each phase. Rather than a fixed plan, it was a living prioritization framework — updated as conditions changed, and designed to keep the team moving on what was possible rather than stalled on what wasn't.

**Shifting the Team's Psychology**

Perhaps the most consequential intervention wasn't logistical — it was cultural. Hassan had to convince a group of academics, operating in the shadow of a significant setback, that completing the build was not just possible but achievable within the timeline. He moved the team from doubt to momentum, aligning every member around a shared sequence of execution rather than a shared anxiety about the deficit.

Once that shift happened, the team moved.

---

## The Outcome

In under one month of on-site construction — starting from a material shortage and with no pre-existing project management structure — the team completed a fully functional **three-bedroom, two-bathroom single-family residence** featuring:

- **Masonry brick construction** sourced and sequenced under field conditions
- **Running water** and full plumbing integration
- **Grid-connected electricity** with an **off-grid sustainable energy system**
- Performance across all ten Solar Decathlon judging categories, including Electrical Energy Balance, Sustainability, and Innovation

**The team won the competition.**

At Africa's first-ever Solar Decathlon, Freedom Builders-sponsored teams delivered a completed, competition-winning home — built under constraint, on time, from a standing start — in front of an international audience of sustainability leaders, academics, and industry professionals.

For Freedom Builders, the result was exactly what it needed: a credible, high-visibility proof of concept that demonstrated the company's ability to deliver in complex, resource-constrained environments.

---

## The Differentiator

The build succeeded not because conditions were favorable — they weren't — but because a structured mind was applied to an unstructured situation. Creating a working project timeline mid-crisis, sourcing materials from a cold start, and getting an academic team to operate at professional construction pace are three distinct skill sets. Deploying all three simultaneously, in a foreign country, in under a month, is what turned a potential public failure into a landmark win.
