## 1. The Foundation: What Makes a White Paper Worth Reading

A white paper fails when it reads like a product brochure disguised as thought leadership. It succeeds when it educates first, builds trust through evidence, and leaves the reader with a new mental model for solving their problem. The frameworks in this document exist to ensure every white paper we produce clears that bar.

The governing principle across all frameworks is this: **education over promotion, clarity over complexity, and narrative over bullet dumps.** The best white papers read like a well-told story, look like a thoughtfully designed magazine article, and leave the reader smarter than when they started.

## 2. Gordon Graham's White Paper Framework ("That White Paper Guy")

Gordon Graham, author of *White Papers for Dummies* and writer of over 300 white papers for companies including Google, Rackspace, and Oracle, provides the most widely-adopted structural framework for white paper creation.

### 2.1 The Three Flavors

Graham identifies three fundamental types of white papers, each suited to a different purpose and audience position in the buying cycle:

**Flavor 1 — The Backgrounder (Vanilla)**
The oldest type of white paper. A backgrounder explains the key features, functions, and benefits of a single product or service. It is most useful late in the buying cycle when prospects are evaluating specific solutions. The structure is straightforward: introduce the offering, walk through its capabilities, and demonstrate how it solves the reader's problem. Think of it as an evaluator's guide or product briefing.

**Flavor 2 — The Numbered List (Strawberry)**
The lightest and most accessible flavor. A numbered list provides a set of points, tips, or concerns about a topic. It works well at any stage of the buying cycle and is ideal for generating awareness or sparking interest. The structure is inherently scannable, making it the easiest flavor for time-pressed readers to consume. Examples: "7 Trends Reshaping Enterprise Security" or "5 Questions Every CTO Should Ask About Zero-Trust Architecture."

**Flavor 3 — The Problem/Solution (Chocolate)**
The most complex and most powerful flavor. A problem/solution white paper recommends a new or improved solution for a persistent business or technical problem. It requires the most extensive research and the strongest evidence. The structure follows a persuasive arc: establish the problem, explain why current approaches fall short, present the recommended solution, provide evidence and case studies, and close with a call to action. This flavor works best in the middle of the buying cycle when prospects are researching options but haven't committed to a vendor.

**Mixing Flavors:** Graham acknowledges that hybrid white papers combining elements of two or more flavors can be effective, but warns against muddying the core structure. The problem/solution backbone is the strongest foundation for hybrids.

### 2.2 The 90/10 Rule

A white paper should be approximately 90% educational content and 10% promotional content. The promotional content belongs at the end, typically in the conclusion and call to action. Readers who feel sold to in the introduction will stop reading. Readers who feel educated through the body will be receptive to a recommendation at the end.

This ratio extends to tone: write like a knowledgeable peer sharing insights, not like a salesperson closing a deal. Graham advises writers to "think like a lawyer, write like a journalist, and promote like a madman" — but only in the final pages.

### 2.3 Graham's 12-Step Process

1. **Assemble the team** — Writer, designer, illustrator, subject matter experts, reviewers
2. **Hold the initial conference call** — Align on purpose, audience, and scope
3. **Prepare the plan** — A one-page brief circulated until all reviewers approve
4. **Gather research** — Company background, web research, expert interviews
5. **Prepare an executive summary** — One page summarizing the proposed direction
6. **Write the first draft** — Follow the chosen flavor's structure
7. **Conduct the first review** — SMEs verify technical accuracy
8. **Write the second draft** — Incorporate feedback, tighten narrative
9. **Conduct the second review** — Reviewers check flow, clarity, and tone
10. **Design and layout** — Apply visual design principles (see Sections 4-6)
11. **Proofread** — Final pass for errors, consistency, and formatting
12. **Publish and promote** — Distribution strategy, landing pages, and follow-up

### 2.4 The Call to Action

Graham specifies that a call to action should appear once, at the end of the concluding summary. It does not need to be long; one sentence can suffice. His formula: "To find out more about how your organization can gain [key benefit explained in the white paper] with [product or company name], [do something specific]." A clear CTA increases conversion rates by over 161% compared to documents without one.

## 3. Narrative Structure: Freytag's Pyramid Applied to White Papers

Gustav Freytag's five-act dramatic structure, originally designed for stage plays, provides a surprisingly effective narrative arc for technical white papers. The adaptation works because readers are human beings who respond to tension, stakes, and resolution — even in a document about API architecture.

### 3.1 The Five Stages Applied

**Stage 1 — Exposition (The Setup)**
Introduce the landscape. Who are the players? What is the current state of affairs? In a white paper, this is your introduction and context-setting. Establish the world the reader lives in, confirm their reality, and signal that you understand their domain.

**Stage 2 — Rising Action (The Problem Deepens)**
Build tension. Show why the status quo is unsustainable. Layer evidence: statistics, industry trends, failure case studies. Each paragraph should raise the stakes. The reader should feel the weight of the problem growing. This is where you earn the right to propose a solution.

**Stage 3 — Climax (The Turning Point)**
Introduce the core insight or solution. This is the "aha" moment — the new mental model, the architectural shift, the methodology that changes everything. In a problem/solution white paper, this is where you present your recommended approach. The climax should feel inevitable given the rising action that preceded it.

**Stage 4 — Falling Action (Evidence and Validation)**
Support the solution with proof. Case studies, benchmarks, architecture diagrams, before/after comparisons. The tension built in the rising action now resolves through evidence. Each exhibit and data point should reinforce the climax's central claim.

**Stage 5 — Denouement (Resolution and Next Steps)**
Bring the reader home. Summarize what they've learned, restate the key insight, and provide a clear path forward. This is where the call to action lives. The reader should leave feeling both educated and empowered to act.

### 3.2 Why Narrative Arc Matters

Research on the picture superiority effect (discussed in Section 5) shows that people remember stories far better than they remember lists of facts. A white paper structured as a narrative arc has measurably higher recall and engagement than one structured as a flat information dump. The arc also creates forward momentum — each section compels the reader to continue to the next.

## 4. Dan Roam's Visual Thinking Framework ("The Back of the Napkin")

Dan Roam's *The Back of the Napkin* argues that anyone can use basic drawings to describe and solve complex business problems. His framework is particularly valuable for white paper exhibits because it provides a systematic method for choosing the right diagram type for any concept.

### 4.1 The Four Steps of Visual Thinking

Every visual communication follows four sequential steps:

**Step 1 — Look:** Collect and screen raw information. Gather all the data, facts, and concepts that might be relevant. Cast a wide net.

**Step 2 — See:** Select and group. Identify patterns, relationships, and hierarchies within the raw information. Decide what matters and what doesn't.

**Step 3 — Imagine:** Manipulate and explore. Ask "what if" questions. Rearrange the grouped information into different configurations. Try different visual representations.

**Step 4 — Show:** Make it clear. Choose the visual representation that communicates the insight most effectively and present it to your audience.

### 4.2 The 6W Visual Codex

Roam maps six fundamental questions to six corresponding visual types. This is the single most useful framework for deciding what kind of diagram to put in a white paper:

| Question | What You're Showing | Diagram Type | Example Use |
|----------|---------------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Who/What** | Objects, people, things | Portrait / Character sketch | System components, stakeholders, personas |
| **How Much** | Quantities, measurements | Chart / Graph (bar, pie, line) | Performance metrics, cost comparisons, survey data |
| **Where** | Position, spatial relationships | Map / Layout diagram | Architecture diagrams, network topology, deployment zones |
| **When** | Sequence, timeline, progression | Timeline / Gantt / Flow | Release phases, sprint sequences, process evolution |
| **How** | Process, mechanism, cause-effect | Flowchart / Process diagram | CI/CD pipelines, decision trees, workflow routing |
| **Why** | Motivation, logic, argument | Equation / Multi-variable plot | Cost-benefit analysis, risk matrices, trade-off comparisons |

**Application rule:** Before creating any exhibit in a white paper, identify which of the 6W questions it answers. Then use the corresponding diagram type. This eliminates the common mistake of using a flowchart when a timeline would be clearer, or a bar chart when a spatial map would communicate better.

### 4.3 The SQVID Framework

Once you know *what* to draw (6W), SQVID helps you decide *how* to draw it. SQVID stands for five pairs of contrasting presentation modes:

**S — Simple vs. Elaborate:** How much detail should the diagram include? An executive audience needs the simple version. An engineering audience may need the elaborate version. A good white paper often includes both: a simple overview diagram followed by an elaborate deep-dive.

**Q — Quality vs. Quantity:** Should the diagram emphasize qualitative relationships (architecture boxes and arrows) or quantitative precision (exact numbers, percentages, measurements)?

**V — Vision vs. Execution:** Is this diagram showing the future state (vision) or the current/practical implementation (execution)? Problem sections use execution diagrams showing current pain. Solution sections use vision diagrams showing the better future.

**I — Individual vs. Comparison:** Should the diagram focus on one thing in detail or compare multiple things side by side? Comparison diagrams are powerful in white papers because they let readers see the delta between old and new approaches.

**D — Delta (Change) vs. Status Quo:** Does this diagram show what changes or what stays the same? Delta diagrams create urgency. Status quo diagrams create comfort. Use both strategically.

### 4.4 The Visual Thinking Codex

The Visual Thinking Codex is the master reference grid that combines the 6W framework (vertical axis) with the SQVID modes (horizontal axis). It produces a matrix of approximately 30 distinct diagram configurations. In practice, you use it by:

1. Identifying which W your exhibit answers (e.g., "How" — process)
2. Choosing the SQVID mode that fits your audience (e.g., Simple + Vision)
3. Looking up the intersection to determine the optimal visual treatment

This codex eliminates guesswork from diagram design and ensures every exhibit in a white paper is purpose-built for its communicative goal.

### 4.5 Why Hand-Drawn Feels Work

Roam's research shows that hand-drawn sketches (or diagrams that feel hand-drawn) invite significantly greater audience participation and engagement than polished corporate graphics. They feel approachable, unintimidating, and honest. For white papers, this translates to a design principle: **prefer clean simplicity over glossy complexity.** A well-labeled box-and-arrow diagram communicates more effectively than a 3D-rendered architectural infographic.

## 5. Garr Reynolds' Presentation Zen Principles

Garr Reynolds' *Presentation Zen* provides design principles originally developed for slide presentations that translate directly to white paper page design, exhibit layout, and information hierarchy.

### 5.1 The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

The most important concept from Presentation Zen for white paper design. The signal-to-noise ratio measures the proportion of relevant content (signal) to irrelevant or distracting elements (noise) in any visual display.

**Maximizing SNR in white papers means:**
- Every element on the page must serve the message. If it doesn't inform, explain, or guide, remove it.
- Decorative borders, clip art, gradient backgrounds, and ornamental dividers are noise.
- Clear headings, purposeful whitespace, well-labeled diagrams, and legible typography are signal.
- A diagram's labels, axes, and data points are signal. Its gridlines, 3D effects, and shadow decorations are noise.

**The degradation test:** Ask of every visual element: "If I remove this, does the reader lose any understanding?" If the answer is no, remove it.

### 5.2 Restrain, Reduce, Emphasize

Reynolds distills visual simplicity into three actions:

**Restrain:** Resist the urge to add more. Every additional element competes for the reader's attention. The hardest design skill is knowing when to stop.

**Reduce:** Look at what you've created and ask what can be removed without losing meaning. Combine elements where possible. Simplify complex diagrams into their essential components.

**Emphasize:** Once you've restrained and reduced, the remaining elements should be unmistakably clear. Use visual weight (size, color, position) to direct the reader's eye to what matters most. Emphasis only works when it's selective — if everything is bold, nothing is bold.

### 5.3 The Picture Superiority Effect

Research demonstrates that information presented as pictures is remembered significantly better than information presented as text alone, especially when exposure time is limited (more than 30 seconds after exposure, pictures dominate recall). Reynolds recommends using pictures and words together, ensuring they reinforce the same information.

**For white papers, this means:**
- Every major concept should have a corresponding visual (diagram, exhibit, or illustration).
- Exhibits should appear adjacent to the text they illustrate, not in an appendix.
- Captions on exhibits should reinforce (not merely repeat) the surrounding text.
- A reader who only looks at the exhibits and their captions should still grasp the white paper's core argument.

### 5.4 Empty Space as Design Element

Reynolds, drawing from Japanese aesthetic principles, argues that empty space (negative space, white space) is not wasted space — it is an active design element that gives the remaining content room to breathe and be understood. White papers that cram every square inch with text and graphics feel overwhelming and are abandoned faster than those with generous margins, paragraph spacing, and breathing room around exhibits.

### 5.5 The Four Design Principles (C.R.A.P.)

Reynolds integrates Robin Williams' four fundamental design principles:

**Contrast:** Make different things visibly different. Headings should be clearly distinct from body text. Exhibit backgrounds should contrast with page backgrounds. Use contrast to create visual hierarchy.

**Repetition:** Repeat visual elements consistently throughout the document. The same heading style, the same diagram color palette, the same caption format. Repetition creates unity and professionalism.

**Alignment:** Every element should have a visual connection to something else on the page. Nothing should be placed arbitrarily. Align text to margins, diagrams to column grids, and captions to their exhibits.

**Proximity:** Group related items together. Place captions near their exhibits. Keep body text near its corresponding heading. Separate unrelated sections with generous spacing. Proximity signals relationship; distance signals separation.

## 6. Edward Tufte's Information Design Principles

Edward Tufte, author of *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* and three subsequent landmark books, provides the authoritative framework for data visualization integrity and clarity. His principles are non-negotiable for any white paper that includes charts, graphs, or data-driven exhibits.

### 6.1 The Data-Ink Ratio

Tufte's most famous concept. The data-ink ratio is the proportion of a graphic's ink devoted to the non-redundant display of data-information. Good graphical representations maximize data-ink and erase as much non-data-ink as possible.

**Tufte's formulation:**
- Data-ink ratio = (data-ink) / (total ink used in the graphic)
- The goal is a ratio as close to 1.0 as possible.

**Practical rules:**
- Above all else, show the data.
- Maximize the data-ink ratio, within reason.
- Erase non-data-ink, within reason.
- Erase redundant data-ink, within reason.
- Revise and edit.

**In a white paper context:** Remove chart borders that don't convey information. Remove background fills on chart areas. Remove grid lines unless they're essential for reading exact values. Remove legends when direct labels are clearer. Remove 3D effects entirely — they always distort data perception.

### 6.2 Chartjunk

Tufte coined "chartjunk" to describe all unnecessary or distracting decorative elements in data visualizations. Chartjunk includes:

- Moire patterns and heavy cross-hatching
- Gratuitous 3D effects
- Pictorial decorations (clip art within charts)
- Excessive gridlines
- Redundant labels and legends
- Ornamental axes and tick marks
- Background images or textures behind data

**The rule:** If an element doesn't help the reader understand the data, it is chartjunk and should be removed. Every decorative element risks obscuring or misrepresenting the underlying numbers.

### 6.3 Graphical Integrity

Tufte lists six principles of graphical integrity:

1. The representation of numbers, as physically measured on the surface of the graph, should be directly proportional to the numerical quantities represented.
2. Clear, detailed, and thorough labeling should be used to defeat graphical distortion and ambiguity.
3. Show data variation, not design variation.
4. In time-series displays of money, deflated and standardized units of monetary measurement are nearly always better than nominal units.
5. The number of information-carrying (variable) dimensions depicted should not exceed the number of dimensions in the data.
6. Graphics must not quote data out of context.

### 6.4 The Lie Factor

The Lie Factor quantifies graphical distortion: Lie Factor = (size of effect shown in graphic) / (size of effect in data). A Lie Factor of 1.0 means perfect accuracy. Greater than 1.0 means the graphic overstates the effect. Less than 1.0 means it understates. Tufte considers any Lie Factor outside the range of 0.95 to 1.05 to be a significant distortion.

**For white papers:** Always verify that the visual size of differences in your charts matches the actual numerical differences. A bar that is twice as tall should represent a value that is twice as large — never use perspective, 3D, or area-based tricks that distort proportions.

### 6.5 Small Multiples

Small multiples are series of the same small graph repeated in one visual, each showing a different slice of the data (different time periods, different categories, different conditions). Tufte advocates strongly for small multiples because they enable rapid visual comparison without the cognitive overhead of switching between separate charts.

**White paper application:** Instead of one cluttered chart with six overlapping lines, create six small identical charts side by side, each showing one line clearly. The reader can scan across them to spot patterns instantly.

### 6.6 Sparklines

Sparklines are data-intense, design-simple, word-sized graphics that can be embedded directly in running text. They show trends and variation in a compact form. Tufte describes sparklines as a kind of "word" that conveys rich quantitative information without breaking the flow of a sentence.

**White paper application:** Use sparklines in comparison tables to show trends alongside numerical values. A table cell showing "Revenue: $4.2M" communicates a fact; the same cell with a sparkline showing the upward trajectory over six quarters communicates a story.

## 7. Cognitive Load Theory and Progressive Disclosure

John Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (1980s) identifies three types of cognitive load that apply directly to white paper design:

### 7.1 Three Types of Cognitive Load

**Intrinsic Load:** The inherent difficulty of the subject matter. A white paper about cryptographic key management has higher intrinsic load than one about project management. You can't reduce intrinsic load, but you can manage it through sequencing and scaffolding.

**Extraneous Load:** Unnecessary cognitive effort caused by poor presentation. Dense paragraphs with no visual hierarchy, inconsistent formatting, ambiguous diagrams, and jargon without definitions all create extraneous load. This is the load you can and must eliminate.

**Germane Load:** The productive effort required to build mental models and integrate new knowledge. This is the load you want to maximize. Clear explanations, well-chosen analogies, and progressive complexity all increase germane load by helping readers build understanding.

### 7.2 Progressive Disclosure in White Papers

Progressive disclosure means revealing information in layers, from overview to detail, allowing readers to access deeper complexity only when they're ready. In a white paper:

- Start each section with a high-level summary before diving into detail.
- Use high-level architecture diagrams before detailed component diagrams.
- Define terms before using them in complex arguments.
- Place detailed technical specifications in appendices rather than the body.
- Structure exhibits so the main insight is visible at a glance, with detail available on closer inspection.

Research shows that bounce rates drop 15-25% on pages implementing proper progressive disclosure versus information-dense alternatives.

## 8. Visual Design System for White Papers

Synthesizing Reynolds, Tufte, and Roam, here is a unified visual design system for white papers:

### 8.1 Color Palette

Apply the **60-30-10 rule** for color distribution:

- **60% — Primary (Navy/Dark):** Headings, emphasis text, diagram borders. Establishes authority and structure.
- **30% — Secondary (Teal/Accent):** Subheadings, diagram fills, callout boxes, table headers. Creates visual interest and guides the eye.
- **10% — Highlight (Amber/Alert):** Key statistics, warning indicators, call-to-action elements. Draws immediate attention to critical information.
- **Body text** should be dark gray (not pure black) on white for optimal readability.
- **Background** should be white or very light gray. Never use colored backgrounds behind body text.

Maintain WCAG accessibility standards: minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio for normal text, 3:1 for large text.

### 8.2 Typography

- **Headings:** A clean sans-serif font (Calibri, Inter, or Helvetica). Bold weight, with clear size differentiation between H1, H2, and H3 levels.
- **Body text:** The same sans-serif family at 11pt, with 1.3-1.5 line spacing. Dark gray color (e.g., #475569) rather than pure black for reduced eye strain.
- **Captions:** Italic variant of the body font, slightly smaller (9-10pt), centered below exhibits.
- **Code/file references:** Bold or monospace to distinguish them from surrounding text.
- **Maximum two font families** in any document. One for headings, one for body (or a single family with weight variation).

### 8.3 Exhibit Design Rules

Every exhibit (diagram, chart, table, or illustration) in a white paper should follow these rules:

1. **Title every exhibit.** Use a consistent format: "Exhibit N: [Descriptive Title]"
2. **Caption every exhibit.** One sentence below the exhibit explaining the key takeaway, not just describing what the exhibit shows.
3. **Reference every exhibit in the body text.** No orphan exhibits — every visual must be introduced and discussed in the surrounding paragraphs.
4. **Use the 6W Codex** to select the right diagram type for each exhibit's communicative purpose.
5. **Apply the data-ink ratio test.** Remove every visual element that doesn't convey information.
6. **Maintain consistent styling.** All exhibits should use the same color palette, font, line weights, and spacing.
7. **Label directly.** Prefer labels on or adjacent to data points over legends that require the reader to look back and forth.
8. **Size appropriately.** Exhibits should be large enough to read comfortably but not so large they dominate the page at the expense of surrounding text.

### 8.4 Table Design Rules

Tables are one of the most information-dense elements in a white paper and deserve special attention:

- Use alternating row shading (very light gray) for readability on tables with more than 5 rows.
- Left-align text columns; right-align numerical columns.
- Bold the header row and use a colored background (secondary palette color) to distinguish it.
- Remove vertical borders between columns — horizontal rules alone are usually sufficient.
- Keep column count to 5 or fewer when possible. More than 5 columns overwhelms most readers.
- If a table exceeds one page, repeat the header row on the continuation page.

### 8.5 Page Layout

- **Margins:** At least 1 inch (2.54cm) on all sides. Generous margins signal quality and give the reader's eye a resting place.
- **Headers:** Document title or section name in small text, right-aligned.
- **Footers:** Page numbers centered. Optional: document version and date.
- **Section breaks:** Use a thin horizontal rule or generous vertical spacing (not decorative dividers) between major sections.
- **Paragraph spacing:** Space after paragraphs (6-12pt) rather than first-line indentation for a modern, professional look.

## 9. Diagram Type Selection Guide

A quick-reference guide mapping common white paper concepts to optimal diagram types, synthesizing Roam's 6W Codex with Tufte's clarity principles:

| Concept to Communicate | 6W Category | Recommended Diagram | Key Design Rule |
|------------------------|-------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| System architecture | Where | Layered block diagram | Label every block; show data flow with arrows; use spatial position to indicate hierarchy |
| Process or workflow | How | Swimlane flowchart | One entry point, clear decision diamonds, labeled arrows, left-to-right or top-to-bottom flow |
| Timeline or phases | When | Horizontal timeline | Equal spacing for equal time periods; milestone markers; phase labels above, details below |
| Performance comparison | How Much | Bar chart (horizontal) | Direct labels on bars; no 3D; sort by value unless there's a natural ordering |
| Trend over time | How Much + When | Line chart | Minimal gridlines; direct line labels; no more than 4-5 lines per chart |
| Component relationships | Who/What | Entity-relationship diagram | Clear boxes with labeled connections; crow's foot or arrow notation for cardinality |
| Decision logic | How + Why | Decision tree | Binary yes/no branches; color-code paths by outcome; leaf nodes show results |
| Risk or priority matrix | Why | 2x2 or 3x3 matrix | Color-coded quadrants; axis labels; items placed as dots or icons within quadrants |
| Before/after comparison | Why (Delta) | Side-by-side panels | Identical layout in both panels; highlight what changed with accent color |
| Data distribution | How Much | Histogram or box plot | Clear bin labels; show mean/median; avoid pie charts for more than 3-4 categories |
| Multi-dimensional comparison | Why | Radar/spider chart or small multiples | Limit to 5-7 axes for radar; prefer small multiples for more than 7 dimensions |
| Stakeholder/persona mapping | Who/What | Icon-labeled portrait grid | Simple icons; name and role labels; group by category |
| Cost or resource breakdown | How Much | Stacked bar or treemap | Direct labels showing percentages; consistent color assignment across related charts |
| Deployment topology | Where | Network/deployment diagram | Clear zone boundaries; icon-based nodes; labeled connections with protocol/port info |

## 10. The White Paper Quality Checklist

Before publishing any white paper, validate it against this checklist synthesized from all four frameworks:

### Content (Graham)
- [ ] The white paper follows one of the three flavors (or a deliberate hybrid)
- [ ] The content is at least 90% educational, 10% or less promotional
- [ ] The problem is established before the solution is introduced
- [ ] Evidence supports every major claim (data, case studies, expert references)
- [ ] The call to action appears once, at the end, and is specific
- [ ] Technical jargon is defined on first use
- [ ] The executive summary can stand alone as a one-page overview

### Narrative (Freytag)
- [ ] The document follows a narrative arc with rising tension and resolution
- [ ] Each section compels the reader to continue to the next
- [ ] The "climax" (core solution/insight) feels earned by the preceding evidence
- [ ] The conclusion resolves the tension established in the introduction

### Visual Design (Reynolds + Tufte)
- [ ] Signal-to-noise ratio is maximized — no decorative elements without purpose
- [ ] Every exhibit has a title, caption, and body text reference
- [ ] Exhibits use the correct diagram type for their communicative purpose (6W Codex)
- [ ] Data-ink ratio is maximized — no chartjunk, no 3D effects, no gratuitous gridlines
- [ ] Color palette follows the 60-30-10 rule with WCAG-compliant contrast
- [ ] Typography uses proper Word styles (Heading 1/2/3, Normal) for easy restyling
- [ ] White space is used generously — pages do not feel cramped
- [ ] All charts have a Lie Factor between 0.95 and 1.05

### Structure (All Frameworks)
- [ ] Table of contents is present and links to all major sections
- [ ] Headers and footers include document title and page numbers
- [ ] Appendix contains detailed specifications that would clutter the main narrative
- [ ] Document uses progressive disclosure (overview before detail)
- [ ] The document can be restyled by modifying Word/Google Docs style definitions without touching individual paragraphs

## 11. The Four Storylines (Dan Roam's "Show and Tell")

In his follow-up book *Show and Tell*, Roam identifies four fundamental storylines that every presentation or document follows. White papers should consciously choose one:

**The Report:** Communicates facts and findings. Best for backgrounder-flavor white papers. Structure: Here is what we found, organized by theme or category.

**The Explanation:** Teaches a new insight or ability. Best for educational white papers that introduce a new methodology or framework. Structure: Here is a concept you don't yet know, built up step by step.

**The Pitch:** Recommends a new action or solution. Best for problem/solution-flavor white papers. Structure: Here is a problem you face, here is why current approaches fail, here is a better way.

**The Drama:** Inspires a new belief or way of looking at the world. Best for thought leadership pieces that challenge industry assumptions. Structure: Here is what everyone believes, here is why it's wrong, here is the new truth.

Most technical white papers are either Explanations or Pitches. Choosing the storyline before writing ensures the narrative arc is coherent from the first sentence.

## 12. Implementation: From Framework to Finished Document

### 12.1 Pre-Writing Phase

1. **Choose the flavor** (Graham): Backgrounder, Numbered List, or Problem/Solution
2. **Choose the storyline** (Roam): Report, Explanation, Pitch, or Drama
3. **Map the narrative arc** (Freytag): Outline the five stages with specific content for each
4. **Plan the exhibits** (Roam 6W + SQVID): For each planned exhibit, identify the W it answers, the SQVID mode, and the resulting diagram type
5. **Define the visual design system**: Color palette, typography, exhibit styling rules

### 12.2 Writing Phase

6. **Write the executive summary first** — This forces clarity of purpose
7. **Write the body following the narrative arc** — Introduction (Exposition) through Conclusion (Denouement)
8. **Draft exhibit captions as you write** — Captions clarify what each exhibit must communicate
9. **Apply the 90/10 rule continuously** — Flag any sentence that sounds promotional and move it to the conclusion

### 12.3 Design Phase

10. **Create exhibits using the 6W Codex** — Match each exhibit to its communicative purpose
11. **Apply the data-ink ratio test** to every exhibit — Remove unnecessary elements
12. **Apply Restrain, Reduce, Emphasize** to every page — Remove visual noise
13. **Verify the Lie Factor** on every data chart — Ensure graphical integrity
14. **Apply C.R.A.P. principles** — Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, Proximity throughout

### 12.4 Review Phase

15. **Run the Quality Checklist** (Section 10)
16. **Test progressive disclosure** — Can a reader who skims only headings and exhibits grasp the argument?
17. **Test the narrative arc** — Does each section naturally lead to the next?
18. **Verify style-based formatting** — All text uses named Word styles, not inline formatting
19. **Final proofread** — Grammar, consistency, and formatting
