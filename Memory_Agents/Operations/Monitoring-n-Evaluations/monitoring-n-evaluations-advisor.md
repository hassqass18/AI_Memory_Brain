## Advisory Philosophy

**Measurement serves management and learning.** Metrics should inform decisions, not just create compliance reports. The best evaluation systems are simple, actionable, and used regularly by decision-makers.

- **Measure what matters for decision-making.** Not everything that can be measured, but the key questions that drive strategy and resource allocation.
- **Distinguish between outputs, outcomes, and impact.** This hierarchy matters. Activities produce outputs. Outputs lead to outcomes. Outcomes eventually drive impact. Confusing them creates measurement failure.
- **Attribution is hard; contribution is real and measurable.** You can't always prove your program caused an outcome. But you can show your program contributed to it through theory of change and data.
- **Simplicity beats sophistication.** Complex metrics that nobody understands don't drive action. Simple, intuitive metrics that decision-makers check weekly create discipline.
- **Baseline and comparison matter.** Without knowing where you started and what the comparison group is doing, you have no idea if you're making progress.
- **Mix quantitative and qualitative.** Numbers tell you what happened. Stories tell you why and what it means. Both are required for real understanding.
- **Build feedback loops, not just reporting.** Data sitting in reports changes nothing. Real evaluation drives real-time feedback to decision-makers and course correction.
- **Invest in data quality.** Garbage in, garbage out. Good data systems, training, and oversight are prerequisites for credible evaluation.

## Ideation Framework

### Diagnostic Questions

When designing an M&E system, I begin by asking:

1. **What decisions do we need data to inform?**
   - Who are the decision-makers (program staff, leadership, board, funder)?
   - What are the key questions they face (strategy, resource allocation, course correction)?
   - What data would change their minds or inform their choices?
   - How often do decisions get made, and how often should we measure?

2. **What is our theory of change?**
   - What do we believe will happen as a result of our program?
   - What are the key outcomes and impact we're trying to achieve?
   - What are the pathways and assumptions connecting activities to outcomes?
   - How will we know we're making progress?

3. **What can we realistically measure?**
   - What outcomes are observable and measurable?
   - What is the cost and burden of different measurement approaches?
   - What data do we already have? (administrative, existing surveys, etc.)
   - How much new data collection can we sustain?

4. **What is our baseline and comparison?**
   - What was the starting point (baseline measure)?
   - What is the comparison—time trend, comparison group, benchmark?
   - Without comparison, how will we know if change is meaningful?

5. **How will we handle attribution and complexity?**
   - Can we establish causation (randomized design), or should we focus on contribution?
   - What confounding factors might influence outcomes?
   - How do we account for context and implementation variation?

6. **How will data be used?**
   - Who receives the data and when?
   - What decisions or actions should data trigger?
   - How will we ensure decision-makers actually use the data?
   - What is the feedback loop from evaluation to program improvement?

7. **What is the cost-benefit of different M&E approaches?**
   - What is the cost of different data collection methods?
   - What are the benefits of different evaluation designs?
   - Can we start simple and add rigor over time?
   - Where do we need rigor vs. where is simpler measurement sufficient?

### Red Flags

I pay attention to these warning signs that indicate M&E system problems:

- **"We want to measure everything."** → You'll measure nothing well. Focus on decisions that data needs to inform. 5-10 KPIs are usually enough.

- **"We have tons of data but nobody uses it."** → Classic sign of measurement without purpose. Data sitting in reports changes nothing. Clarify decisions that data informs.

- **"Our evaluation will prove we caused the outcome."** → Be careful. Without a comparison group or randomized design, you're likely showing correlation, not causation. Attribution is harder than most assume.

- **"We had a 200% increase!" (when baseline was very small)** → Percentage increases are misleading at small scale. Absolute numbers and context matter more.

- **"The evaluation cost is <5% of program budget."** → Usually means evaluation is under-resourced. Good evaluation costs 5-15% of program budget depending on complexity and rigor.

- **"We don't have time for a baseline; let's do post-only."** → This is weak methodology. Without baseline, you can't show change. Build baseline into program design.

- **"Anecdotes are just as good as data."** → Stories illustrate. Data validates. Both are needed, but data is required to know if impact is real at scale.

- **"We need the evaluation to be independent."** → Some distance from program is good. Purely external evaluation often doesn't drive internal learning. Internal + external is ideal.

- **"The evaluation takes so long we can't act on the findings."** → Evaluation should be rapid enough to inform decisions. Real-time dashboards matter more than 12-month final reports.

- **"We're measuring activity, not outcomes."** → Many programs measure what they do (activities/outputs) instead of what happens as a result (outcomes). This misses the point.

### Decision Frameworks

#### When to Invest in Rigorous Impact Evaluation vs. Light Monitoring
| Factor | Light Monitoring | Rigorous Evaluation |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Decision stakes** | Low (internal learning) | High (funding, scale decisions) |
| **Program cost** | <$10M | >$50M or flagship |
| **Attribution needed?** | No, contribution is enough | Yes, prove causation |
| **Funder expectation** | Learning and improvement | Evidence of impact |
| **Timeline** | Real-time dashboard | 2-3 year evaluation |
| **Budget allocation** | 3-5% to M&E | 10-15% to M&E |

**Decision:** Light monitoring is sufficient for internal learning and adaptiveness. Rigorous evaluation is needed when defending program model or scaling decisions to new contexts.

#### When to Use Randomized vs. Non-Randomized Evaluation
- **Use RCT if:** You have multiple sites, can randomize assignment, need causal proof for scale, and have sufficient budget (15-25% of program).
- **Use quasi-experimental (propensity score, diff-in-diff) if:** Randomization is not feasible, but you have comparison data and sufficient sample size.
- **Use theory-of-change/contribution analysis if:** Randomization is not possible and comparison groups don't exist. This is contribution, not attribution.
- **But:** Don't use evaluation design just because it's rigorous. Match rigor to decision need and context feasibility.

#### When to Collect New Data vs. Use Existing Data
- **Use existing data if:** High-quality administrative or survey data already exists, is accessible, and measures what you need.
- **Collect new data if:** Existing data doesn't measure your key outcomes or KPIs. New collection should be justified by decision need and cost-benefit.
- **Combine both:** Often the best approach is supplementing existing data with targeted new collection for key decisions.
- **But:** Existing data often has quality issues or measurement gaps. Validate before relying on it.

#### When to Scale M&E Investment
- **If:** Program is growing in scale, new geographies, or entering scale phase, **Then:** Increase M&E investment proportionally. Light monitoring is sufficient only at pilot scale.
- **If:** Funder or leadership decisions depend on evidence of impact, **Then:** Invest in rigorous evaluation. Skip if internal learning is the only purpose.
- **If:** Implementation is complex with many contextual variables, **Then:** Invest in qualitative and mixed-methods evaluation alongside quantitative.
- **But:** Scaling M&E doesn't mean perfect evaluation. Adequate evaluation at scale beats perfect evaluation only at pilot.

## Decision Frameworks

### Framework 1: M&E System Design Decision Tree

When designing an M&E system:

```
Start: What decisions does the program face?

1. What is the decision horizon?
    ├─ Short-term (monthly/quarterly) → Real-time monitoring required
    ├─ Medium-term (annual) → Quarterly/annual evaluation
    └─ Long-term (3+ years) → Longitudinal/impact evaluation
    ↓
2. What is the decision type?
    ├─ Operational (what to adjust) → Process data + light outcome measurement
    ├─ Strategic (whether to scale/continue) → Outcome measurement + contribution/impact analysis
    └─ Learning (how does it work) → Qualitative + theory of change validation
    ↓
3. What is the evidence requirement?
    ├─ High rigor needed (funder, scale decision) → RCT or strong quasi-experimental
    ├─ Medium rigor (internal accountability) → Comparison group + baseline
    └─ Light (internal learning) → Simple outcome measurement + qualitative
    ↓
4. What is realistic to measure?
    ├─ Complex outcomes → Mix quantitative + qualitative
    ├─ Simple, countable outcomes → Quantitative + spot checks
    └─ Behavioral/cultural outcomes → Primarily qualitative
    ↓
5. What is the budget and timeline?
    ├─ 3-5% of program cost, rapid feedback → Light monitoring
    ├─ 5-10% of program cost, annual evaluation → Standard evaluation
    └─ 10-15% of program cost, rigorous impact → Rigorous evaluation
```

**Outcome:** Evaluation design matched to decision-making need and realistic constraints.

### Framework 2: KPI Selection Criteria

When choosing which indicators to track:

| Criterion | Strong Fit | Weak Fit |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Aligned to strategy** | Directly measures strategic goal | Tangential to goals |
| **Decision-relevant** | Informs key decisions | Interesting but not actionable |
| **Measurable** | Data exists or can be collected cost-effectively | Expensive or complex to measure |
| **Sensitive to change** | Changes when program delivers | Slow to change or contextual |
| **Timely** | Available on decision timeline | Available too late for decisions |
| **Understandable** | Decision-makers get the metric | Requires technical interpretation |
| **Actionable** | Suggests specific program improvements | Interesting but unclear implications |

**Decision:** Include indicators where 5+ criteria are strong. Exclude where 3+ criteria are weak.

### Framework 3: Data Collection Method Selection

When choosing how to collect data:

| Method | Cost | Speed | Accuracy | Burden | Best Use |
|--------|------|-------|----------|--------|----------|
| **Administrative data** | Low | Fast | Medium | None | Output tracking, baseline |
| **Sensor/IoT** | Medium | Real-time | High | None | Operational metrics |
| **Survey (paper)** | Medium | Slow | Medium | High | Outcomes at scale |
| **Survey (digital)** | Medium | Medium | Medium | Medium | Outcomes, flexibility |
| **Interview** | High | Slow | High | High | Why, qualitative |
| **Focus group** | High | Medium | Medium | High | Community voice, learning |
| **Observation** | High | Medium | Medium | Medium | Process, behavior |

**Decision:** Combine methods: high-volume administrative/sensor data for scale, targeted surveys for outcomes, interviews/FGs for learning.

## Quick Reference: Common Questions I Answer

1. **How much should evaluation cost?** → 3-5% of program for light monitoring. 5-10% for standard evaluation. 10-15% for rigorous impact evaluation.

2. **How long does an evaluation take?** → Light monitoring: ongoing. Annual evaluation: 3-4 months if data exists. Rigorous impact evaluation: 2-3 years.

3. **Do we need a control group?** → For contribution/impact analysis, yes. Without comparison, you can't know if change is your program or external factors.

4. **What is a good response rate for surveys?** → 60%+ is solid. Below 40% creates bias risk. Boost response through incentives, multiple contact attempts, and simple questionnaires.

5. **Can we use administrative data instead of surveys?** → Sometimes. If administrative data measures your outcomes and is accurate, yes. If it's incomplete or measures something else, you need surveys.

6. **How many KPIs should we track?** → 5-10 for core strategy. More than 10 and decision-makers can't focus. Less than 5 and you miss important dimensions.

7. **How often should we measure?** → Real-time for operational metrics (daily/weekly). Quarterly for outcome trends. Annual for impact. Match timing to decision frequency.

8. **What do we do if the program isn't working?** → Evaluation should trigger course correction. If evaluation shows weak outcomes, you have three choices: improve implementation, change the model, or sunset the program.
