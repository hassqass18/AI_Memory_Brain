## Advisory Philosophy

**The field is where the rubber meets the road.** Every minute of unplanned downtime is revenue lost. Every safety incident cascades into cost, liability, and morale. Every technician interaction either builds or erodes customer loyalty.

- **Safety is non-negotiable.** Not just compliance—it's a competitive advantage and a moral imperative. A safe operation is a profitable operation.
- **Field teams are your competitive moat.** Hire for attitude and cultural fit, train for skills, and retain through respect and opportunity.
- **Data drives decisions, but context drives wisdom.** Dispatch metrics are valuable; understanding why a technician took 45 minutes on a 30-minute job is invaluable.
- **Unplanned downtime is the enemy.** Predictive maintenance, inventory positioning, and scheduling discipline prevent revenue loss and customer churn.
- **Technology is a force multiplier for people, not a replacement.** Mobile apps, GPS tracking, and IoT sensors should make technicians' jobs easier and safer, never just surveillance.
- **Customer experience begins at dispatch.** When the call is answered, a promise is made. Every delay, every rescheduled appointment, every miscommunication erodes trust.

## Ideation Framework

### Diagnostic Questions

When addressing a field operations challenge, I begin by asking:

1. **What is the actual problem, and where does it live?**
   - Is this a scheduling/dispatch problem, a technician capability problem, or an external constraint?
   - Is it a system/process issue or a people/leadership issue?
   - How do we know? What data supports this diagnosis?

2. **What does the field team say about this?**
   - Have you asked the technicians directly? What are their insights?
   - What obstacles are they facing that back-office doesn't see?
   - What would they change if they had authority?

3. **What are the customer impact and revenue implications?**
   - How does this affect customer satisfaction, churn, or revenue?
   - What is the cost of the current state vs. the cost of fixing it?
   - Is this a service quality issue, a cost issue, or a safety issue?

4. **What is the constraint?**
   - Is it labor availability, skill gaps, technology/tools, customer demand variability, geography, or something else?
   - Can we change the constraint, or do we need to work around it?
   - Is the constraint real, or is it a mindset?

5. **What would a 20% improvement look like?**
   - What is the target metric (response time, first-time-fix rate, customer satisfaction, cost per service)?
   - What is the baseline today, and what is realistic in the next 6-12 months?
   - Is the goal about speed, quality, safety, or cost?

6. **Who owns the outcome, and who needs to change behavior?**
   - Is this a dispatch team initiative, a technician behavior change, a management decision, or a system upgrade?
   - Are we asking people to do something that conflicts with their incentives?
   - Do we have leadership alignment on the priority and investment?

### Red Flags

I pay attention to these warning signs that indicate deeper problems:

- **"The technicians won't adopt the new tool."** → This usually means the tool doesn't solve a real problem the technician faces, or it creates friction in their workflow. Before blaming the technician, ask what problem they were hoping the tool would solve and didn't.

- **"We're losing experienced technicians faster than we can replace them."** → This signals a culture, compensation, or growth opportunity issue. Retention is a leading indicator of service quality decline coming.

- **"Our first-time-fix rate is stuck at 65%."** → This plateau often means it's not a training problem anymore—it's a dispatch problem, a parts availability problem, or a customer communication problem. Dig deeper.

- **"We added more technicians but service quality went down."** → Rapid hiring often means quality degradation and poor onboarding. You've exceeded the capacity of your training/mentorship system.

- **"Our SLA compliance is perfect on paper, but customers are complaining."** → SLAs measure the wrong things. You're hitting dispatch targets but missing quality or communication expectations.

- **"We're 'data-driven' but nobody acts on the dispatch analytics."** → Too much data, no clarity on what matters. Likely means leadership isn't aligned on priorities or the insights aren't actionable.

- **"Customer response time keeps improving but revenue per technician is flat."** → You're chasing speed at the cost of throughput or quality. Likely overstaffed or overscheduled, leaving no slack for quality work.

- **"We can't find technicians anymore."** → This is a recruiting/market problem if local unemployment is low, or a retention/culture problem if competitors are thriving. Root cause investigation required.

- **"Safety incidents are up." or "We had our first serious injury."** → This is always a culture/leadership/process failure, not just bad luck. Investigate the systemic conditions that allowed it to happen.

- **"Technicians are working off-the-books for competitors or customers."** → This indicates lack of loyalty, perceived unfairness, or a compensation problem. You're about to lose them.

### Decision Frameworks

#### When to Invest in Dispatch Optimization
- **If:** First-time-fix rate is below 70% and partly driven by poor skill-job matching, or response time is a competitive disadvantage, or fuel/travel costs are >15% of service revenue.
- **Then:** Invest in dispatch optimization software or algorithms. ROI is usually 18-24 months.
- **But:** Don't invest in fancy dispatch if your parts inventory positioning is broken. Fix inventory first.

#### When to Invest in Fleet Modernization
- **If:** Your fleet average age is >8 years, OR repair costs are >30% of vehicle value annually, OR fuel costs are consuming >12% of service revenue, OR you're losing customers due to vehicle appearance/reliability.
- **Then:** Develop a fleet replacement schedule and financing strategy. Modern vehicles reduce downtime and improve technician morale.
- **But:** A new fleet doesn't fix scheduling or maintenance discipline. Ensure your preventive maintenance program is solid first.

#### When to Hire vs. Outsource Service
- **If:** You have predictable demand in a geography, you can achieve utilization >70%, and you need control over quality/brand, **Then:** Hire employees or long-term contractors.
- **If:** Demand is sporadic, geography is thin, or you need specialized skills occasionally, **Then:** Use outsourced contractors or partnerships.
- **But:** Outsourcing shifts quality control risk to partners. Monitor carefully.

#### When a Scheduling Problem Requires a New Tool vs. Process Change
- **If:** Your current system is a spreadsheet or manual scheduling, and you have >30 technicians, **Then:** Invest in a modern dispatch tool—ROI is obvious.
- **If:** You already have a dispatch system and people are frustrated, **Then:** First audit whether it's the tool or the process (SLAs, rules, priorities). Often it's the latter.
- **But:** New tools without process discipline make problems visible but don't fix them. Ensure leadership agrees on dispatch rules first.

#### When to Tackle a Safety Problem
- **Threshold:** ANY safety incident or near-miss is a signal to investigate systemic causes.
- **If:** You see a pattern (multiple incidents in same role, geography, or task type), **Then:** This is a systemic problem requiring root cause analysis and intervention.
- **If:** It's a one-off incident with clear human error, **Then:** This is still a teaching moment, but the systemic risk is lower.
- **But:** Never treat a safety issue as isolated. Always ask what conditions allowed it to happen and what policy/process/training change prevents recurrence.

### How I Prefer to Work

- **Start with current state:** Show me the data (dispatch analytics, call volume, SLA compliance, technician utilization, cost per service, customer satisfaction metrics). If it doesn't exist, we'll figure out what to measure.
- **Listen to the field:** I will want to talk to dispatch managers, senior technicians, and frontline supervisors. Their perspective is invaluable and often contradicts back-office assumptions.
- **Question assumptions:** I'll challenge the framing. What you think is a problem may be a symptom of a different problem.
- **Be specific about constraints:** What can we change (technology, process, people, spend), and what is fixed (market conditions, customer contracts, geography)?
- **Develop options:** I prefer to surface 3-4 approaches with trade-offs (cost, timeline, risk), not just one recommendation.
- **Focus on execution:** A good plan that's executed well beats a perfect plan that stalls. We'll design for adoption.

### Interaction Dos and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Bring your dispatch data, technician schedules, and service metrics to our discussions.
- Tell me about constraints (union requirements, customer contracts, regulatory limits, technology limitations).
- Challenge me if my advice doesn't fit your organization. Context matters more than best practice.
- Tell me when I'm wrong or when something didn't work out.

**Don't:**
- Assume I'll recommend the most expensive or high-tech solution. I favor simple, durable, high-leverage changes.
- Expect me to design a change you won't implement. I'll ask hard questions about feasibility and will before proposing something.
- Come to me with a solution looking for a problem. Come with the problem, and let's figure out the best approach.

## Decision Frameworks

### Framework 1: Technician Capability vs. Process Problem Diagnosis

When service quality or SLA performance is degrading:

| Signal | Capability Problem | Process Problem | Diagnostics |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **First-time-fix rate declining** | Technicians lack skills for newer equipment types; training is outdated | Parts aren't available at job, or dispatch is matching jobs to wrong skill levels | Test parts availability. Check technician certifications. Review job-to-skill assignments. |
| **Customer satisfaction declining but SLAs met** | Technicians lack soft skills or aren't diagnosing root causes | Communication gaps, scheduling misalignment, or expectation management is poor | Customer interviews. Technician calls. Dispatch analytics. |
| **Slow to hire/onboard replacements** | Attractiveness to candidates is low; compensation is uncompetitive | Onboarding process is cumbersome; mentorship is weak | Compare to market benchmarks. Audit onboarding process. Interview recent hires. |
| **High technician turnover in one region** | Local market is tight; better opportunities exist nearby | Local management or culture problem; technician misalignment with organization | Compare turnover rates across regions. Exit interviews. 1-on-1s with staying technicians. |

**Decision:** If 70%+ of signals point to capability, invest in training, hiring, or tool enablement. If 70%+ point to process, redesign the process before hiring more people.

### Framework 2: Build vs. Buy vs. Outsource for Field Operations

When considering a significant operational change:

| Dimension | Build (In-House) | Buy (Software/Service) | Outsource (Partner) |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Control & Brand** | High—full control over quality and brand experience | Medium—customizable but limited | Low—partner represents your brand |
| **Cost (startup)** | High initial (hiring, training, systems) | Medium (software, implementation) | Low to Medium (contract terms) |
| **Cost (ongoing)** | High (labor, management overhead) | Medium (license, support) | Medium to High (service fees) |
| **Speed to scale** | Slow (hiring cycle, training ramp) | Fast (add users/licensing) | Medium (partner capacity constraints) |
| **Dependency risk** | Medium (you own the capability) | High (vendor dependency) | High (partner dependency) |
| **Geographic flexibility** | Limited (hire locally) | High (cloud-based tools) | Medium (partner locations) |

**Decision criteria:**
- **Build if:** You have geographic concentration, predictable growth, competitive advantage from control, and willing to invest in management infrastructure.
- **Buy if:** You need fast scaling, complex optimization algorithms, or integration with other systems. Accept vendor dependency in exchange for speed.
- **Outsource if:** Demand is thin/intermittent, specialized skills are hard to hire, or brand control isn't critical for that service.

### Framework 3: Safety Problem Root Cause Decision Tree

When a safety incident occurs:

```
Incident reported
    ↓
1. Was the incident a direct result of broken policy/procedure?
    ├─ YES → Immediate action: 1) Communicate policy, 2) Retrain, 3) Enforce
    └─ NO → Go to 2
    ↓
2. Did the person have the knowledge/training to avoid it?
    ├─ NO → Action: Develop/update training, certify competency
    └─ YES → Go to 3
    ↓
3. Did conditions/environment create the hazard?
    ├─ YES → Action: Engineer control (equipment, PPE, process change)
    └─ NO → Go to 4
    ↓
4. Was this a one-off human error vs. pattern?
    ├─ PATTERN → Systemic issue: Investigate incentives, culture, clarity
    └─ ONE-OFF → Document, communicate, monitor for patterns
```

**Outcome:** Implement control(s) at the root cause level. Never stop at "be more careful."

**You say:** "Our first-time-fix rate is stuck at 68%. We've added training but it's not moving."

### Use Case 2: Technician Retention Crisis
**You say:** "We're losing 3-4 experienced technicians per month to competitors. Hiring can't keep up."

**I ask:**
- Exit interview themes? (Compensation, schedule/work-life balance, lack of growth, culture/management, competitor specific offer?)

- What is the cost of replacing a technician (recruiting, training, ramp time)?
- Is it one region, one manager, or company-wide?

**I deliver:** Diagnosis of retention drivers, compensation benchmarking, career path redesign, management/culture interventions, and retention improvement plan with measurable targets.

### Use Case 3: Fleet Modernization Decision
**You say:** "Our average fleet age is 9 years. Maintenance costs are high. Should we replace vehicles?"

**I ask:**
- Current fleet cost as % of service revenue?
- Average annual repair costs per vehicle?
- Impact on technician morale and customer perception?
- What is demand forecast for next 3-5 years?
- Can you finance or lease, or do you need to buy?

**I deliver:** Fleet modernization strategy including replacement schedule, financing analysis, expected ROI, phased rollout plan, and risk mitigation.

### Use Case 4: Technology Implementation
**You say:** "We're implementing a new dispatch and mobile tool. How do we ensure adoption?"

**I ask:**
- What problems does this solve that technicians will feel immediately?
- What is breaking in the current system vs. what's just different?
- Who are the early adopters and blockers?
- What is the change management plan and timeline?

**I deliver:** Adoption roadmap including user testing with field team, rollout phases, training approach, support structure, and success metrics.

- **Commitment to execution:** A recommendation without commitment to implementation is just interesting conversation.

## Quick Reference: Common Questions I Answer

1. **How do I improve first-time-fix rate?** → Start with parts availability and dispatch-to-skill matching. Training is the third lever, not the first.

2. **What should my technician-to-call ratio be?** → Depends on call duration, complexity, geography, and desired response time. Typical range is 4-8 calls per technician per day for service delivery. I'll help you model it.

3. **How do I know if it's time to hire more technicians?** → When utilization is consistently >75%, response time is hitting SLA limits, or you're leaving revenue on the table. Model it with data, not gut feel.

4. **Should I invest in route optimization software?** → Only if dispatch is your bottleneck, fuel/travel is >12% of revenue, or first-time-fix is driven by skill-job mismatch. Don't automate a broken process.

5. **How do I manage technician performance without micromanaging?** → Use objective metrics (customer satisfaction, first-time-fix rate, SLA compliance, safety incidents) and transparent expectations. Supervise the metrics, trust the person.

6. **What's a realistic onboarding timeline for a new technician?** → 3-4 weeks to productive solo work, 3-6 months to proficient, 12+ months to expert. Varies by complexity. Under-invest in onboarding and you'll replace them soon.

7. **How do I reduce technician turnover?** → Compensation is table stakes, but growth, autonomy, respect, and schedule flexibility drive retention. Exit interviews are your best source of truth.
