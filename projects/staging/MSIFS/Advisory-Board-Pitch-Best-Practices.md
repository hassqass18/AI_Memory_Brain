**Prepared:** 2026-04-05
**Purpose:** Research foundation for approaching potential advisory board members for the Frontier Impact Infrastructure & Minerals Fund ($200M)

## 1. How to Approach Potential Advisory Board Members

### Warm Introductions vs. Cold Outreach
Warm introductions significantly outperform cold outreach. Warm intros achieve 10–34% reply rates vs. 2–10% for cold emails. 85% of advisory and professional roles are filled through personal connections. For MSIFS, this means leveraging existing LP relationships, DFI networks, and GP team connections first. When cold outreach is unavoidable, use existing investor relationships to request introductions rather than going direct.

### Timing and Channels
Send outreach on weekdays, mid-morning or early afternoon in the recipient's time zone. Avoid holidays, earnings periods, or major policy announcements. Wait 5–7 days before following up; reference the prior message rather than starting fresh.

### MSIFS-Specific Channels
- Tap existing LP and co-investor networks (DFIs: IFC, AfDB, EBRD, ADB)
- Leverage GP team's East Africa and Central Asia networks
- Target professionals from organizations active in Kenya, Tanzania, Mozambique, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan (e.g., Frontier Energy, EAAIF, AIIF4, KAZ Minerals)
- Use industry conferences: CIEL, Africa Finance Corporation events, AIFC forums

## 2. What to Include in an Advisory Board Pitch

### Lead With
- Fund thesis and market opportunity — be specific about infrastructure gaps in target regions
- Impact potential — quantify jobs created, infrastructure access, community benefit
- Institutional backing — LP names, DFI interest, co-investment partners where possible
- GP team credentials — frontier market experience, relevant exits, regional expertise

### Core Pitch Structure (Keep Initial Outreach Under 200 Words)
1. Personalized opening referencing their specific background
2. Fund thesis in 1–2 sentences
3. Why their expertise is specifically needed (gap you're filling)
4. Clear ask: role, time commitment, specific responsibilities
5. Compensation structure (transparent from the start)
6. Single call-to-action with 2–3 time slots

### What NOT to Include
- Vague promises about equity without specifics
- Generic language applicable to any fund
- Excessive technical detail that obscures the thesis
- Pressure tactics or artificial urgency

## 3. What Motivates People to Join Advisory Boards

### Primary Motivations (in priority order)
1. **Mission and Impact Alignment** — especially powerful for frontier market infrastructure; advisors want to influence development outcomes in underserved regions
2. **Prestige and Association** — association with a well-funded institutional fund enhances advisors' own credibility
3. **Access to Deal Flow** — early visibility into $5–15M infrastructure and minerals deals
4. **Network Expansion** — access to institutional capital, co-investors, and fellow advisors
5. **Compensation** — equity and/or cash retainer (details below)

### Profile-Specific Motivations
- **Ex-Government / Policy Officials:** Policy impact, regulatory influence, legacy building in target regions
- **Industry Operators:** Deal flow, co-investment opportunities, sector network value
- **DFI Professionals:** Impact and mission alignment, blended finance innovation
- **Institutional Investors:** Portfolio diversification, co-investment access, learning about frontier asset classes
- **Sector Experts (Infrastructure/Minerals):** Application of expertise, commercial opportunity

## 4. Personalizing the Ask by Advisor Profile

### Ex-Government Officials / Policy Experts
- Open with: regulatory impact and the opportunity to shape investment frameworks
- Emphasize: government partnerships secured, policy discussions underway (Kenya/Kazakhstan)
- Specific ask: "Guidance on navigating regulatory dynamics in [Country]"
- Compensation signal: impact alignment first, equity secondary

### Industry Operators / Sector Experts
- Open with: commercial opportunity and deal flow in their area of expertise
- Emphasize: specific sectors (Lamu Port, Kazakhstan minerals, Mtwara LNG)
- Specific ask: "Operational expertise to assess deals and improve portfolio performance in [sector]"
- Compensation signal: meaningful equity stake (0.5–0.7%) that aligns interests with fund returns

### Institutional Investors / Capital Sources
- Open with: fund performance strategy, competitive positioning, return distribution approach
- Emphasize: co-investment pipeline, institutional quality of fund governance
- Specific ask: "Perspective as we build institutional investor relationships and co-investment partnerships"
- Compensation signal: cash retainer preferred over equity for this profile

### DFI / Development Finance Professionals
- Open with: impact thesis and SDG alignment of infrastructure investments
- Emphasize: community engagement, job creation, technology transfer potential
- Specific ask: "Help refine our impact measurement framework and navigate DFI co-financing structures"
- Compensation signal: mission alignment primary, moderate equity secondary

## 5. Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Recruiting for Name Recognition Over Engagement** — A well-known but disengaged advisor actively harms credibility during LP due diligence. Always assess willingness to participate before extending an offer.

2. **Listing Advisors Before Written Agreement is Signed** — Never include someone in pitch materials without a signed advisory agreement. Always confirm in writing first.

3. **Failing to Address Conflicts of Interest** — Advisors serving competing funds or portfolio companies create fiduciary issues. Require disclosure in the advisory agreement.

4. **Settling for Accessible Candidates Over Best Fit** — Invest time identifying ideal candidates. Be patient; relationship-building before the ask pays off.

5. **Vague Expectations and Commitments** — Define measurable obligations: "2–3 LP introductions per quarter" or "attendance at quarterly advisory meetings with 48 hours' notice." Vague agreements deteriorate.

6. **Not Providing Advisors With Resources** — Share fund documents, market analysis, and investment updates. Advisors can't contribute without context.

7. **Misaligned Compensation for Involvement Level** — Calibrate equity/cash to actual expected involvement. Token equity for a deeply involved advisor destroys motivation.

## 6. Best Practices for the Initial Outreach Message

### Subject Line
- Strong: "Advisory Board Opportunity — MSIFS East Africa Infrastructure Fund"
- Avoid: "Exciting Opportunity" / "Quick Question" (too generic)

### Message Structure
| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| Opening | Personalized reference to their specific expertise/background |
| Context | Fund name, size, thesis (1–2 sentences) |
| Why Them | Specific gap their expertise fills |
| The Ask | Role, time commitment, what you need from them |
| The Offer | Compensation (equity %, vesting, retainer if applicable) |
| CTA | "20-minute call — are Tuesday or Thursday next week open?" |

### Tone Principles
- Respectful of their time — acknowledge their schedule
- Specific, not generic — name the exact expertise you need
- Warm but professional — not overly formal, not casual
- Mutual benefit framing — explain what they gain, not just what you need

### Example Opening Framing (MSIFS)
> "Given your [X years in East African infrastructure / government experience in minerals policy / operational expertise in Kazakhstan's energy sector], I believe you would be an exceptional fit for our fund's advisory board. MSIFS is raising $200M to invest in critical infrastructure and minerals across East Africa and Central Asia, and your expertise in [specific area] would directly address one of our key gaps as we enter [country/sector]."

## 7. Follow-Up Cadence and Relationship-Building

### Pre-Recruitment (2–3 Months Before the Ask)
- Share relevant articles, market reports, or insights with target advisors
- Ask for their input on a specific fund strategy question (no strings attached)
- Build the relationship before making the formal ask

### After Initial Outreach
- Day 1: Initial message sent
- Day 5–7: First follow-up (brief, reference prior message)
- Day 14–21: Second follow-up with new piece of value (article, fund update)
- Day 30+: Longer pause, then re-engage with fresh angle if still no response

### After Recruitment
- Quarterly advisory meetings (in-person minimum once per year, ideally in target market)
- Monthly brief updates (1–2 pages) on fund pipeline, key decisions, and market developments
- Clear meeting agendas sent 48 hours in advance; action items within 24 hours
- Annual in-person meeting in East Africa or Central Asia for field exposure

### Relationship Maintenance
- Solicit advisor input on real decisions — demonstrates respect for their expertise
- Create peer networking between advisors at board meetings
- Celebrate milestones: successful investments, portfolio company progress, LP wins
- For impact-focused advisors: provide regular impact metrics alongside financial updates

## 8. Language That Works — Framing the Ask

### Core Principle: Ask for Advice First
"If you ask for money, you get advice. If you ask for advice, you get money." The same applies to advisory recruitment: frame the ask as seeking their wisdom, not seeking a favor.

### Strong vs. Weak Framing

| Profile | Weak | Strong |
|---|---|---|
| Government/Policy | "We need help with regulatory issues" | "We're establishing the model for how infrastructure funds work within Kenya's regulatory framework — your expertise is crucial to getting this right" |
| Industry Operator | "Will you provide strategic guidance?" | "You understand this sector more deeply than almost anyone. We'd like your help identifying the right deals and operational approaches in [specific sector]" |
| Institutional Investor | "Join our advisory board" | "We'd benefit from your perspective as we structure our co-investment program and expand our institutional LP relationships" |
| DFI Professional | "Help us create impact" | "We believe infrastructure investment can be both financially attractive and genuinely transformative for East African communities. We need advisors who share this conviction and can help us prove the model" |

### Language Principles
- **Specificity:** Name the exact area where their expertise applies
- **Mutual benefit:** Explain what they gain (access, influence, compensation, network)
- **Time respect:** Acknowledge their schedule; be clear about actual time commitment required
- **Legacy framing:** For frontier markets, emphasize the opportunity to shape outcomes in important regions
- **Clear ask:** End with a specific, unambiguous request

## 9. Advisory Board Compensation Structures

### Equity Compensation
| Involvement Level | Equity Range | Vesting |
|---|---|---|
| Specialized/Deep Expertise | 0.5% – 1.0% | 4-year, 1-year cliff |
| General Thought Partnership | 0.15% – 0.35% | 4-year, 1-year cliff |
| MSIFS Recommended (Core Advisors) | 0.5% – 0.7% | 4-year, 1-year cliff |

### Cash Compensation
- Annual retainers: $15K–$50K (varies by seniority and involvement)
- Meeting fees: $2K–$5K per quarterly meeting (for senior advisors)
- Specific project fees: $10K–$25K for deep-dive market or investment assessments
- Expense coverage: Travel, meals, accommodation for all advisory meetings (standard)

### Hybrid Structures (Recommended for MSIFS)
- Core Advisors: 0.5% equity + $20K annual retainer
- Specialized Experts (e.g., Central Asia minerals): 0.7% equity + $15K annual retainer
- Institutional Investors (who prefer cash): 0.25% equity + $30K annual retainer

### Additional Value Elements
- Co-investment rights (highly valued — advisors gain access to deals in hard-to-access markets)
- Portfolio company board seats (for operationally engaged advisors)
- Impact participation metrics for development-focused advisors

## 10. Top PE/VC Advisory Board Structure Benchmarks

### Board Size and Composition
- Emerging/specialty funds ($100M–$300M): 3–5 advisors optimal
- Mix: industry operators, former government officials, institutional investors, sector specialists
- Geographic diversity matching fund's investment thesis is essential

### Governance Standards
- Formal written advisory agreements before any public mention
- Clear defined scope: advisory boards advise, do not vote or make binding decisions
- Minimum quarterly meeting cadence (in-person minimum annually)
- Conflict of interest disclosure and recusal procedures required
- Named advisory board chair to facilitate meetings and report to GP

### Advisory Board Functions (Best Practice)
- Deal sourcing and vetting support
- Portfolio company operational guidance
- Strategic input on market trends and competitive positioning
- Investor relations facilitation (co-investors and new LPs)
- Independent governance perspective
